1. The Lows (aka, the Yunga, the wilderness)

functional
The low-shacks, built by ex-soldiers and immigrants, often lack power and running water. The name is more to do with their status than their height: some are as many as three or four stories. Structural collapses, even fatal ones, are not uncommon.
Despite the relative poverty of its inhabitants, the Lows are an essential part of Pengerrand. They are where everybody, rich and poor alike, go to get their car repaired, listen to live music, watch sports matches, eat the most interesting food. Crime, an an informal sense, is low; the people are concerned with maintaining the spirit (they might say the “art") of pangatoi, de-escalation, conflict resolution, compromise: literally, “listening". There is little violence and comparatively little theft. Violations of law are somewhat common, but they are most often of the form of licensing violations and tax evasion. The bureaucracy of the city is not much respected.

2. The Graffiti

dysfunctional
One of the few forms of “crime” that is persistent is graffiti, across all neighborhoods. The “Critique” (the common name for the Pengerrand Metropolitan Committee for Assessment of Quality of Spontaneous Community-Led Aesthetic Modification) has at least caused the more unappealing forms of vandalism to decrease.

3. The Mayor's Office

Conflict with: The Lows.
dysfunctional
The Mayor almost always has an adversarial relationship with the Yunga. For historical reasons, the tournament of elections that result in the selection of the Mayor leave the Yunga with effectively about a third as many votes as citizens north of the river.
Since it represents only a minority of the city, the Mayor often enacts policies that turn out to be either unpopular (because they are in conflict with certain interests) or ineffective (because they fail to take into account hidden complexities with the minority are unaware of).

4. The Red Marsh

highly functional
An area around the Penger river, spanning both the north and south banks. Designated as a park by the city. There are often floating market-boats on the Yunga side, and walkways on the north.
It is a quiet and pleasant place to be on a mild summer evening, watching a sunset. The blood petrels tend to eat all the nuisance insects, and the only ones that remain are the various species of fireflies that glow orange, and pink, and red, giving the marsh its name.

5. Central Art Supply

Supported by: The Graffiti
dysfunctional
A store just south of the river, in a commercial neighborhood. It’s rather old compared to its immediate neighbors, having been passed down in a family for a few generations now. Vandals from around the city and suburbs buy their paint and printing materials here.
Constantly in conflict with the city’s zoning administration, who have been somewhat more successful in trying to put it out of business for failure to pay taxes. The shambling mess of architecture that the store sprawls across is starting to show its age. Roofs and interior pipes leak, damaging supplies.

6. The Ministry of Architecture

Conflict with: The Lows
highly dysfunctional
New construction is meant to be approved by the ministry, but illegal construction is far more common than legal in the sprawl of the yunga.
The ministry is a husk of what it was a century hence: Pengerrand began its current iteration a century and a half ago as an experiment in trying to enforce consistency in design, in imitation of the renovations in Urrakeny, but the practices did not take root.

7. Utsbak Slopes

functional
Some neighborhoods on the south bank of the Penger, not far from the Marsh, that are a bit more middle-class than the Lows.
The area considered the “slopes” is expanding somewhat in recent years to the neighboring flats. There’s a thriving cluster of Gorgobyan restaurants at the western fringe of it.

8. Erosion near the Penger River

highly dysfunctional
The river grows a little in width every year as more soil is washed away. The residents of the Lows migrate more easily, but the once high-value houses on the north bank are threatened. Some neighborhoods have been gutted and essentially turned into a northern version of the lows. The houses themselves (those that haven’t been fortified) are unsafe, in the process of collapse, though it doesn’t deter the squatters.

9. Builders & Planners

Conflict with: The Ministry of Architecture
dysfunctional
The trade union has different priorities from the ministry; they want their folks to get paid, and to not get maimed by falling beams. They could care less about aesthetic cohesion. In fact they prefer a bit of variety, and if fashion mandates you knock down your house and build one that’s more current, so much the better.
Nobody really pays dues anymore, so the headquarters is looking a bit shabby. The bylaws are ignored, and the chairpersonship is determined mostly by seniority, and has little actual power. The organization as a whole still has some cohesion: its members look out for one another.

10. Immigration

Supported by: The Lows
functional
Nice weather, plentiful cheap (albeit flimsy and dilapidated) housing, a ready market for extranational trinkets and cuisines, and a generally cosmopolitan welcomingness toward strangers make Pengerrand a beacon for immigrants.
Immigration is easy and well-supported by the state. Permanent residency is granted for almost all foreign citizens without a criminal record — and even some of those if the crimes are determined to be political ("targoroi") rather than civil ("manoi") or moral ("sukemjot") in nature — on a yearly renewing basis, and full citizenship after as little as three years. Two of the last three mayors were not born in Pengerrand (but were born to somewhat wealthy families elsewhere)
