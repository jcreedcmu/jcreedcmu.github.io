1. Opal Graves

functional
A historical graveyard where several famous figures from history are buried. Not many places are still available, except to the very rich and well-connected. Cremation is much more popular these days besides.
It functions more like a park than a graveyard. The paths are well-maintained, and the scenery is attractive, if morbid. It tends not to bother the residents. No particularly separate rules of decorum apply here, which horrifies some foreign visitors.

2. Yarminna Paper Factory

highly dysfunctional
Imnereskne was a city of letters fifty years ago: writers flocked here, and printing businesses thrived. Yarminna had a considerable empire built on vertical integration, despite the absence of a monopoly in any particular segment. Nonetheless they cut down their own forests, printed their own paper, bound their own books, and hired the most popular serial writers, and paid them by the pound. They did well for a while, but war came from the west, and their forests, their factories and their offices were expropriated or destroyed. The paper factory was the one exception: but with the rest of their business gutted, it fell into disuse.

3. Ammatun Drugs

functional
A small neighborhood drugstore in East Byangon. They mainly sell various sandwiches and soups and dumplings, pain remedies, digestive snacks, desserts, ket patches, toilet paper, and fruit. They also do a little under-the-table savings-and-loan business for the regulars, with very reasonable interest rates. Business has picked up since the dairy store across the street closed up due to the owner dying childless.

4. Street Drummers

highly dysfunctional
Of all the buskers and street musicians, the drummers are the biggest nuisance: they leave their gourds out to rot and attract wild pigs, and needless to say they make the most cacophonous noise. Usually the guitarrists at least have some talent, but the rise of drumming as a central focus of Imnereskne music, as well as the affordability of miscellaneous objects that might count as percussion instruments, means that just about everyone, talented or not, makes an attempt.

5. A Most Agreeable Neighbor

dysfunctional
A restaurant that serves fried dough, potato desserts, spiced steak, honey wine, and other local specialties. It’s commonly known to be a money-laundering front for the Babokaba, a criminal organization whose influence is diffused over the peninsula, and which is somewhat in decline lately. Sparsely populated as always, some of the menu items have started to be unavailable due to the absence of enough funds coming in.

6. Highway 200

highly dysfunctional
The ring road that encircles Imnereskne, constructed a few decades ago in an attempt to mitigate traffic jams on the more major radial roads, has its wayfinding signage covered in graffiti and other projects of vandalism.
Entire segments of it are unusable, having been scavenged for reusable chunks of concrete. At best, one has to keep an eye out for stationary carts and settlments. The traffic jams on the spoke roads and downtown are about as worse as ever.

7. Ghost Stories

Supported by: Yarminna Paper Factory
highly functional
Children often tell stories of the paper factory being haunted. You are not supposed to wear anything but gray clothing when exploring its interior.
Morbid stories of mythological creatures — in states of death, or of a nature that defies categorization into alive and dead — are central to the storytelling traditions in Imnereskne. The principal function of a story is, according to these traditions, to take someone out of the mundane world, to enter into a space where humans are not necessarily welcome. The protagonist typically enters a cave, or hole, or goes down a well, or climbs a hill, or opens a door that seems to lead nowhere — or is attacked without provocation — or commits a terrible crime while ket-mad and undergoes a transformation as punishment.

8. White Bark and Green Branches

functional
A retirement home adjoining a hospital. Its courtyard has a variety of colorful plants, originally foreign, but bred to be adapted to the relatively undramatic seasons on the bay coast. There is a pond with blue and yellow fish. Its name is a reference to a novel — there is a quote by Hamr on a plaque near the entrance, about the end of life being the time appropriate to take all of one’s experiences, and synthesize them into a coherent whole through writing. Hamr’s four novels were all written in a span of three years after he turned seventy-one. White Bark and Green Branches was the last of these, after which he only wrote some poetry whose structure is still fairly disputed by scholars that study him.

9. Plaza of the Statue of 2 Taprekkut

highly functional
It’s not entirely clear why an underwhelming Juta monarch (of Negima birth) deserved a statue here in the fist place — but it is revered as a sort of magnet of artistic potential. Other statues appear, and even are immune from the erstwhile impulse of the city to coat every surface with permanent marks, as a sort of commentary. For some reason the rule is suspended here. It is a civic shrine.

10. The Round

highly functional
The round is located in the heart of the city, and here is where all significant events take place. Concerts are common, especially of percussionists. The arena is well-suited for competitive sports when it is not raining. The temperature is sufficiently high year-round for some sort of sports, but the choice varies seasonally. Famous protests have converged here — politicians have spoken here before their election, rallying the — perhaps about ten or fifteen thousand — in attendance.
Unlike many other event venues on the continent, it is a matter of principle that attendance fees are not charged per se. Money is made from food, from merchandise, from insurance, from security services. Tickets are sold, but to be shown later as “proof of attendance", even if you never attended.
